# PHASE 4: TRIANGULATION & HYPOTHESIS VALIDATION

Triangulation was conducted by integrating inferential statistical testing (Chi-square, ANOVA) with unsupervised clustering patterns (K-Means segmentation). This dual-layer validation approach ensured that hypothesis testing was not reliant solely on p-values, but also supported by structural behavioral grouping within the population.

---

## H1: Awareness vs Lifestyle Risk

**Hypothesis H1:**
Individuals with lower health awareness will exhibit higher lifestyle risk behaviors such as poor diet and physical inactivity.

### 1️⃣ Statistical Evidence

* Chi-square test: **p = 0.1031**

The association between awareness category and lifestyle risk category did not reach conventional statistical significance at α = 0.05. This suggests that, at the aggregate level, awareness alone may not be a sufficiently strong predictor of lifestyle risk classification.

### 2️⃣ Cluster-Level Structural Evidence

K-Means clustering revealed distinct behavioral profiles:

* **Cluster 2**: Entirely low awareness population, split between high and low lifestyle risk (48.6% vs 51.4%), indicating heterogeneity within low-awareness individuals.
* **Cluster 3**: Dominated by high awareness individuals, yet 57.9% still fall under high lifestyle risk.
* **Cluster 1**: Substantial proportion of low-awareness individuals with high lifestyle risk (35.2%).

### Integrated Interpretation

While inferential testing does not confirm a statistically significant association, cluster analysis reveals that low awareness groups are frequently embedded within high-risk behavioral profiles. However, high awareness does not consistently translate into low lifestyle risk.

**Conclusion:** H1 receives partial structural support but lacks strong inferential statistical confirmation. Awareness alone does not deterministically explain lifestyle risk behavior.

---

## H2: Age vs Cautious Health Behavior

**Hypothesis H2:**
Older individuals are more likely to demonstrate cautious health behavior compared to younger individuals.

### 1️⃣ Statistical Evidence

* Age vs Action: Chi-square p = 0.1247 (not significant)
* Age vs Urgency: ANOVA F = 7.23, p = 7.71e-05 (highly significant)

While action-taking behavior does not significantly differ across age groups in categorical terms, urgency levels show statistically significant variation.

### 2️⃣ Cluster-Level Evidence

* **Cluster 3** shows higher representation of individuals aged 41–60 and 60+, with greater proportions of proactive behavior.
* **Cluster 2** is dominated by younger age groups (18–25, 26–40) with predominantly passive action patterns.
* Older age groups consistently show higher proactive percentages relative to passive within structured clusters.

### Integrated Interpretation

Although categorical action differences are not statistically significant, urgency levels significantly increase with age. Cluster structure further indicates that older individuals are more concentrated within proactive behavioral segments.

**Conclusion:** H2 is supported in terms of urgency behavior and cluster-based proactive dominance, though categorical action alone does not show statistical significance.

---

## H3: Symptom Awareness but Delayed Medical Consultation (Cognitive Paradox)

**Hypothesis H3:**
There exists a distinct population group that possesses symptom awareness but delays medical consultation.

### 1️⃣ Statistical Evidence

* Awareness vs Action Chi-square: **p = 1.81e-40**

This extremely significant result confirms a strong association between awareness and action-taking behavior.

### 2️⃣ Cluster Identification of the Paradox Group

Clusters 0 and 3 both contain substantial proportions of:

* High Awareness
* Passive Action

For example:

* Cluster 0: 38.97% High Awareness – Passive
* Cluster 3: 38.48% High Awareness – Passive

These clusters demonstrate that awareness does not uniformly translate into proactive consultation behavior.

### Integrated Interpretation

The coexistence of high awareness with passive action confirms the presence of a behavioral-cognitive gap. The statistical association is strong, but cluster analysis reveals that a non-trivial subgroup demonstrates awareness without medical urgency.

**Conclusion:** H3 is strongly supported both statistically and structurally, validating the existence of the “awareness–action paradox.”

---

## H4: Meaningful Behavioral Segmentation

**Hypothesis H4:**
The population can be segmented into meaningful behavioral clusters based on awareness, medical risk, lifestyle risk, and action-taking behavior.

### 1️⃣ Statistical Evidence

* ANOVA (Cluster vs Awareness Score):
  F = 5794.48, p < 0.001

This indicates extremely strong differentiation between clusters in terms of awareness scores.

### 2️⃣ Structural Coherence

Each of the four clusters demonstrates clear behavioral dominance patterns exceeding 60% concentration in specific dimensions:

* Cluster 2: Low awareness dominance
* Cluster 3: High awareness with mixed lifestyle risk
* Cluster 1: Elevated lifestyle risk concentration
* Cluster 0: Mixed awareness but structured action patterns

The segmentation is not random noise; it reflects consistent multidimensional behavioral structuring.

### Integrated Interpretation

The clustering solution demonstrates high internal coherence and statistically significant differentiation across awareness scores, supporting the existence of meaningful behavioral archetypes within the population.

**Conclusion:** H4 is strongly supported. The four-cluster structure represents distinct and interpretable behavioral segments.

---

# Overall Triangulation Outcome

This study demonstrates that:

* Not all hypothesized relationships are uniformly supported by inferential statistics (H1, partial H2).
* Behavioral complexity is better captured through combined inferential and unsupervised modeling approaches.
* The awareness–action gap (H3) emerges as the most statistically robust and structurally consistent finding.
* Population segmentation (H4) provides a validated behavioral framework for targeted intervention strategies.

In summary, triangulation strengthened interpretative validity by integrating statistical testing with behavioral segmentation, preventing overreliance on p-values while avoiding purely descriptive conclusions.
